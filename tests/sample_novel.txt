The Lantern and the Tide

Mara grew up in a fishing village where the sea took more than it gave. Her
father vanished in a storm when she was nine, and the villagers said the deep
had claimed him for a debt no one remembered owing. Mara never believed it. She
kept his old brass lantern on the windowsill and lit it every night, certain
that one day its light would guide him home.

Years passed. Mara became the village's most fearless diver, hauling up nets
and secrets alike from the cold green water. The other villagers grew wary of
her — she swam too deep, stayed under too long, and spoke of lights moving far
below the surface, lights that no honest fish could make.

One autumn, a stranger arrived: a cartographer named Idris, mapping the coast
for a distant king. He carried charts that showed an island where Mara's maps
showed only open sea. When she laughed at him, he showed her a drawing in the
margin — her father's lantern, sketched from memory by a sailor who swore he had
seen it burning beneath the waves.

Together they sailed to the empty place on the map. A storm rose, as storms
always did, and their boat was dragged down into a whirlpool of black water.
But instead of drowning, they fell through into a hollow world beneath the sea —
a drowned city of coral towers lit by a thousand lanterns, each one a soul the
tide had taken.

There, among the lantern-keepers, Mara found her father. He was older, quieter,
bound by a promise he had made to keep the lights burning so that no drowned
soul would ever be lost in the dark. He could not leave; the city would go dark
without a keeper, and the dark would rise to swallow the world above.

Mara understood then what her nightly ritual had truly been — not a child's
hope, but a thread of light stretched across the years between them. She made a
choice. She would take her father's place, and send him home to the village that
still remembered his name.

Idris carried her father back through the whirlpool to the surface, with a new
map in his hands and a promise on his lips. And on a windowsill in a small
fishing village, an old brass lantern began to burn again — lit, this time, from
the other side of the tide, by a daughter keeping the dark at bay so the living
above could sleep.

Some nights, the fishermen say, you can still see her light moving far below the
water, steady as a heartbeat, guiding the lost ones home.
